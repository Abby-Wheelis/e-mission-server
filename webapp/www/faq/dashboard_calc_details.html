Dashboard calculation details

Carbon footprint calculation

Computing the carbon footprint of transportation is tricky to do in sufficient detail. At a high level, the computation involves two components.
  1. The distance travelled
  2. The energy consumed per unit distance
We also provide two comparison metrics to frame this number in a larger context. The US outlined ambitious goals for CO2 reduction of 50% below 2005 levels by 2030 and net-zero by 2050. We use the baseline 2005 GHG emission estimates and 2050 carbon credits to compute the US-wide carbon budget for those years. We then use population growth estimates to compute the per-capita GHG emission target for those years, assuming all sectors decarbonize equally.

GHG emission table

The GHG emission table below uses energy values from the Transportation Energy Data Book (TEDB). It converts them to emission values by:
  • using the EIA carbon dioxide equivalents for fossil fuel vehicles
  • using the EPA eGrid dataset for Colorado (retrieved Spring 2021) for electric vehicles
This is the same calculation that is used for the aggregate dashboard metrics, with the exception of the per-trip rebalancing cost of shared micromobility. Shared micromobility is a small fraction of the mode share in this project, so the impact of the omission is small.

energy/PMT = e; 1 mile = 1.6 km; energy/PkmT = e/1.6 lbs/energy = l; energy/PkmT = e/1.6; lbs/PkmT = (e * l)/1.6 lbs/PkmT = (e * l)/1.6; 1 kg = 2.2 lb; kg/PkmT = (e * l)/(1.6 * 2.2)

The taxi/uber/lyft value requires some explanation. The TEDB has a value for demand response which matches the taxi mobility model, but combines data from cars and small vans. So, we use the driving mode, with an additional penalty for deadheading. Current estimates indicate that 50% of TNC travel is deadheading. We assume the driving EI and load factor for the productive travel and add on an equivalent distance with a load factor of 1 for deadheading. This gives us an overall value of 7214 BTU/PMT = 2840 (driving BTU/PMT) + 4374 (driving BTU/VMT). We use the car VMT, not the mean of car and truck as we do for the other automobile modes, since taxis are typically not trucks.

Comparison data

Current US goals, per "The Long Term Strategy of the United States" are:
  1. 50% reduction below 2005 levels by 2030. 2005 levels are ~ 2Gt (Figure 4). So the 2030 transportation sector goal is 1 Gt
  2. 100% net zero by 2050. The net zero goal relies on around 1Gt of negative emissions (page 6, figure ES-2). So the actual emissions target is 1Gt in 2050. The transportation sector accounts for 29% of current emissions (page 30). Assuming that all sectors decarbonize proportionally, the transportation sector emissions should be ~ 0.3 Gt in 2050.
US population estimates and projections are from the International Population Database (https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/international-programs/about/idb.html). The "Tables" view is particularly useful to obtain the granular estimates that we need. We estimate the per capita values using:

per capita kg CO2e/wk = (1,000,000,000 * 1000 * GTCO2e) / (population * 52)

Calorie Calulations

We use calorie calculations based on Metabolic Equivalents (MET) from the Compendium of Physical Activities. The standard compendium does not include e-bikes, so we use this recent article for e-bikes only.
